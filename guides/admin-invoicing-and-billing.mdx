## Overview

Fieldgrid’s billing model has two main layers:

- **Operational invoicing** – invoices you send to your customers for jobs and work orders.
- **Platform billing** – your own Fieldgrid subscription (plans, seats, modules, trials, payment status).

This guide covers both from the admin perspective.

## Entry points

Admins typically reach these areas from the main navigation:

- **Invoices** for customer billing.
- **Billing** for your own Fieldgrid subscription, plan, seats, and modules.

## Operational invoicing flow

### 1. Create an invoice

When it’s time to bill a customer:

1. Open the **Invoices** section.
2. Choose **Create invoice**.
3. Select the customer and the work you’re billing for.
4. Choose the **invoice type** or template that matches how you bill (for example, time and materials or fixed price).
5. Add or review line items such as:
   - Labor (often pulled from approved time entries).
   - Materials and parts (often pulled from recorded inventory usage).
   - Fixed fees, call‑out charges, or change orders.
   - Discounts, retainers, or prepayments.
6. Review taxes, totals, and due dates.

### 2. Review and send

Before sending an invoice, admins can:

- Preview how it will look to the customer.
- Confirm line items and totals.
- Download or share the final version.

Once finalized, the invoice can be marked as sent and tracked through its lifecycle.

### 3. Track status

After sending, admins can track invoice status such as:

- Draft.
- Sent.
- Paid.
- Overdue.

Invoices can also be connected back to the jobs, work orders, and materials that generated them.

## Step‑by‑step flows for common invoice types

### Time & Materials (T&M) job

Use this pattern when you bill based on **actual time and parts** used.

1. From **Invoices**, select **Create invoice** and choose **Time and materials** (or the closest option your workspace provides).
2. Pick the **customer** and the **job or work orders** you’re billing for.
3. In the invoice builder:
   - Pull in **approved time entries** for the selected job.
   - Pull in **approved parts and materials usage** from inventory.
4. Review the automatically created line items:
   - Group by **labor type** (for example, standard vs. overtime) if you use different rates.
   - Group or summarize **materials** in a way that makes sense for your customer.
5. Add any extra line items:
   - Trip fees.
   - Diagnostics.
   - Miscellaneous charges that didn’t come from time or inventory.
6. Double‑check:
   - Rates, quantities, and tax behavior.
   - That the date range for included time and materials is correct.
7. Save as a **draft**, then send or export when ready.

This flow relies heavily on **Technician time tracking** and **Technician inventory and expenses** being used consistently.

### Fixed‑price job

Use this pattern when the customer has agreed to a **flat fee** regardless of how much time or material you used.

1. From **Invoices**, create a new invoice and choose a **fixed price** or **lump‑sum** template.
2. Select the **customer** and related **job or project**.
3. Add a small number of clear line items such as:
   - “Fixed price – Install rooftop unit (per contract)”.
   - “Commissioning and handover”.
4. Optionally link internal details (time entries, parts) for your records, even if they are not itemized for the customer.
5. Review:
   - The agreed price and any taxes.
   - Any retainers or prepayments that should be applied.
6. Save as draft and send when the job is complete and accepted.

In this model, time and inventory are still important for **profitability**, but you may not expose that detail directly on the invoice.

### Mixed or change‑order billing

Some work is mostly fixed‑price with **extra T&M** components (for example, unexpected repairs or change orders).

1. Start from a **fixed‑price** style invoice.
2. Add the core contractual line item(s) as a lump sum.
3. For each approved change or out‑of‑scope item:
   - Pull in related time entries and materials.
   - Add a separate **T&M line item** or short group (for example, “Change order #2 – Additional ductwork, labor and materials”).
4. Clearly label which lines are:
   - **Included in the original scope**.
   - **Approved extras**.
5. Confirm the customer‑facing description matches any signed change‑order documents.
6. Finalize and send once all changes are agreed.

This mixed approach keeps invoices clear while still honoring the way the work evolved.

## Walking from source data to invoice lines

When Fieldgrid is used end‑to‑end, an invoice is the last step in a chain:

1. Technicians **clock time** against work orders (see **Technician time tracking**).
2. Technicians **request and consume parts**, and log **expenses** (see **Technician inventory and expenses**).
3. Admins **review and approve**:
   - Time entries (hours, rates, job links).
   - Inventory usage (quantities, items, job links).
   - Expenses (receipts, categories, job links).
4. In **Admin inventory management** and time views, admins correct any obvious mistakes before they hit billing.
5. In **Invoices**, admins:
   - Choose which jobs, time, and materials to include.
   - See suggested line items generated from the approved data.
6. Admins make final adjustments (grouping, wording, rounding) before sending the invoice.

When you can, prefer **fixing data at the source** (time entries, inventory, expenses) instead of only editing the invoice. That keeps reporting and job costing accurate.

## Scenarios and playbooks

### Scenario: Standard T&M service job

A customer calls in for a service visit on an existing system.

1. The coordinator:
   - Creates a **job and work order** for the service call.
   - Assigns a technician and schedules the visit.
2. The technician:
   - Clocks in and travels to the site.
   - Uses **Technician daily workflow** to follow checklists and capture notes.
   - Logs time and records any parts used.
3. The admin:
   - Reviews time and parts, approving or correcting them.
   - Creates a **Time and materials** invoice linked to that job.
   - Pulls in all approved time, parts, and expenses for that visit.
4. The customer:
   - Receives a clear invoice that matches what actually happened on site.

You can repeat this pattern for most break/fix or one‑off service calls.

### Scenario: Flat‑fee project with change orders

A customer signs a contract for a multi‑day installation at a fixed price, with an agreement that changes are billed separately.

1. The project manager and admin:
   - Create a **project** and related **jobs** for each major phase.
   - Attach the signed scope or proposal in the project resources.
2. Technicians:
   - Track time and parts normally, even though the main price is fixed.
   - Flag potential scope changes in logs and notes.
3. The admin:
   - For work inside the original scope, keeps internal time and materials as a **profitability check**.
   - For approved changes, creates separate **change‑order jobs or work orders**.
4. When billing:
   - Creates a fixed‑price invoice line for the original scope.
   - Adds additional T&M lines for each change order, using linked time and inventory for backup.

This gives you a clean customer‑facing invoice while preserving enough detail for internal review.

## Platform billing flow

### 1. Trials and conversion

When a new organization joins Fieldgrid, it may start with a trial. During this period, admins can:

- See how many trial days remain.
- Choose when to upgrade.
- Review what plan or features make sense for their team.

If the trial ends before the account is upgraded, Fieldgrid will prompt the admin to choose a paid plan before continuing with certain features.

### 2. Upgrades, seats, and modules

In the billing area, admins can:

- Compare plans.
- Adjust seat counts as the team grows.
- Turn on additional modules when needed.
- Review pricing before confirming changes.

### 3. Payment-required and subscription issues

If there’s a billing issue, such as a failed payment or an expired trial, Fieldgrid will clearly explain:

- What happened.
- Which features may be affected.
- What the admin needs to do next to restore full access.

## Best practices for admins

### Structure items and rates so invoices stay readable

- **Name items clearly** so customers understand what they’re paying for without needing an internal codebook.
- Use **categories** (for example, Labor, Materials, Fees, Discounts) to keep long invoices navigable.
- Where possible, link items to your **inventory catalog** so descriptions stay consistent and usage flows through cleanly.
- If customers don’t need every nut and bolt itemized, group small parts into a single, well‑named line (for example, “Miscellaneous materials – per service visit”).

### Correct source data first when you can

When you notice mistakes while billing, ask:

- **Is this a typo in how we worked, or just how we’re presenting it?**
  - If a technician assigned time to the wrong job, fix the time entry and re‑approve.
  - If a part quantity is wrong, correct the inventory usage so both stock levels and invoices are right.
  - If you simply want to word an item differently for the customer, you can edit the invoice description without changing the underlying data.

As a rule of thumb:

- Fix **time, parts, and expenses** at the source when they are objectively wrong.
- Adjust **invoice grouping, wording, or pricing** when you just need customer‑friendly presentation.

### Reconcile regularly, not just at month‑end

To avoid surprises:

- Set a **cadence** (for example, daily or a few times per week) where admins:
  - Review new time entries for approval.
  - Approve or correct part usage and expenses.
  - Convert completed jobs into draft invoices.
- At **week‑end**:
  - Check for unbilled completed jobs.
  - Look for unapproved time or expenses that would block invoicing.
- At **month‑end**:
  - Reconcile outstanding invoices versus completed jobs.
  - Spot patterns where jobs frequently require corrections and tighten upstream processes.

This rhythm keeps operational work, financials, and Fieldgrid data tightly aligned.
