## Overview

Fieldgrid’s inventory module lets admins:

- Track items and stock levels across locations.
- Approve and record inventory transactions.
- Connect inventory usage to jobs and work orders.

## Entry points

Admins usually access inventory from the main navigation, and they may also jump into it while reviewing jobs, work orders, or technician requests.

## Key screens

- **Inventory overview**
  - Shows:
    - Item catalog.
    - Current stock by location.
    - Key metrics or alerts.
- **Part requests and approvals**
  - Surfaces for:
    - Pending part requests from technicians.
    - Approval or rejection flows.
- **Inventory detail views**
  - Item-level detail pages or side panels showing:
    - Transaction history.
    - Linked jobs/work orders.

## Core workflows

### 1. Maintain the item catalog

- Create and manage inventory items:
  - Item name and code.
  - Category.
  - Units of measure.
  - Default locations and thresholds.
- Fieldgrid helps admins keep the catalog clean and consistent so technicians can find the right parts quickly.
- Depending on how your team works, you may also be able to import items in bulk.

### 2. Record and approve inventory transactions

- Transaction types typically include:
  - Receipts (stock coming in).
  - Issues (stock going out).
  - Adjustments (corrections).
- Admins can:
  - Create new transactions.
  - Approve or reject proposed transactions.
- Transactions are linked to:
  - Jobs and work orders (for consumption).
  - Warehouses or stock locations.

### 3. Handle part requests from technicians

- Technicians can request parts from the field.
- Those requests:
  - Appear in admin views for triage and approval.
- Admins can:
  - Approve, partially approve, or reject.
  - Convert approved requests into inventory transactions.
  - Notify technicians of statuses.

### 5. Manage the inventory lifecycle for an item

From the moment you add an item to when it is fully consumed, you can follow a simple lifecycle:

1. **Create the item**
   - Add it to the catalog with a clear name, code, and category.
   - Set default locations and, if needed, minimum stock thresholds.
2. **Receive stock**
   - Record receipts as inventory transactions, including:
     - Quantity and location.
     - Reference to a PO or vendor, where applicable.
3. **Issue or allocate stock**
   - Convert technician requests into issues.
   - Link those issues to jobs or work orders so you know where items went.
4. **Reconcile and adjust**
   - When counts don’t match:
     - Perform cycle counts or spot checks.
     - Use adjustments to correct quantities, adding notes for audit history.
5. **Review usage patterns**
   - Look at transaction history for:
     - High‑volume items that drive a lot of cost.
     - Items that frequently go short or obsolete.

Thinking in terms of this lifecycle helps you keep inventory accurate and ready for billing.

### 4. Reporting and reconciliation

- Admins can review:
  - Historical transaction reports.
  - Stock valuation or variance reports (where supported).

## Scenarios and playbooks

### Scenario: Large parts‑heavy job

A job is expected to consume a lot of material, possibly across multiple visits.

1. The admin:
   - Reviews the scope and creates or updates catalog items for all major parts.
   - Optionally creates a **staging location** or dedicated stock for the project.
2. As parts arrive:
   - Records **receipts** into the relevant warehouse or staging area.
3. As technicians work:
   - Approve **part requests** linked to the job.
   - Convert them into issues from the staging location to the field.
4. Throughout the job:
   - Monitors stock levels and any variance between expected and actual usage.
5. At billing:
   - Uses transaction history to ensure that all consumed materials appear on invoices in a way that makes sense to the customer.

This pattern keeps large, high‑value jobs from becoming a black box of material spending.

### Scenario: Technician bought materials on the way

Sometimes technicians need to buy something en route to the job.

1. The technician:
   - Logs an **expense** for the purchase with:
     - A short description (for example, “PVC fittings – local supplier”).
     - The amount and receipt photo.
     - The related job or work order.
2. The admin:
   - Reviews the expense for correctness.
   - Decides whether to:
     - Treat it purely as an expense, or
     - Also create an inventory item/transaction so it can be reused or tracked.
3. In inventory:
   - If the material is a one‑off, you may skip cataloging it and let it flow through as a **pass‑through expense**.
   - If it’s something you’ll stock going forward, add it to the catalog and record a **receipt**.
4. At billing:
   - Include the cost as:
     - A line item (for example, “Materials purchased – as agreed”), or
     - A detailed part entry if it’s now in the catalog.

This pattern keeps ad‑hoc purchases visible while nudging your team toward more standardized inventory over time.

## Best practices for admins

### Keep the catalog clean and searchable

- Use **consistent naming and categories** so technicians can quickly find items.
- Avoid duplicate items with slightly different names—merge or retire them where possible.
- Add brief descriptions when the part name alone might be unclear in the field.

### Decide when something belongs in inventory vs. as a direct expense

- Treat frequently used or reusable items as **inventory**:
  - Add them to the catalog.
  - Track receipts and issues carefully.
- Treat true one‑off or low‑value purchases as **expenses**:
  - Log them with enough detail for billing and reporting.
  - Avoid cluttering the catalog with items that will never be used again.

### Reconcile inventory alongside billing

- Align your **inventory review cadence** with your **billing cadence**:
  - When you close out a batch of jobs, also review related inventory usage.
  - Make sure high‑value materials have clear, linked transactions.
- Use variance reports or spot checks to:
  - Catch missing or unrecorded issues.
  - Reduce write‑offs before they snowball.
