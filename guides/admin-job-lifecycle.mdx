## Overview

Jobs and work orders are at the heart of Fieldgrid’s operations model. This guide walks through the **admin job lifecycle**:

- Creating jobs under projects.
- Breaking work into work orders.
- Assigning technicians.
- Tracking progress and closing out work.

## Entry points

Admins usually start from:

- The **Jobs** section in the sidebar.
- Project or customer views that link into filtered lists of jobs.

## Lifecycle steps

### 1. Create or import jobs

From the Jobs list:

- Click **Create job**.
- Provide:
  - The customer and project this job belongs to.
  - A clear job name and description.
  - Start and target completion dates.
  - Status and priority.
  - A lead technician, if applicable.

Some admins may also import multiple jobs at once from a structured file, depending on your setup.

### 2. Define work orders

Within a job, admins break work into **work orders**:

- Each work order represents a concrete task or visit.
- Work orders typically include:
  - A title and short description.
  - Which jobsite they’re for (if there are multiple).
  - Assigned technician(s).
  - Due date, priority, and status.

For more complex work, you can add subtasks or checklists to make expectations clear.

### 3. Assign technicians and schedule work

Admins can assign technicians either:

- Directly on the job or individual work orders.
- Through the scheduling view, using a calendar or board‑style interface.

This ensures each technician knows exactly which work orders they own and when they’re expected to be on site.

### 4. Track progress and collaboration

In the job details view, admins can see:

- Which work orders are still to‑do, in progress, or done.
- Which technicians are assigned and where they are working.
- Any time logged against the job.
- Checklists, site logs, comments, and files captured by the team.

This creates a single place to understand how a job is progressing and what, if anything, is blocked.

### 5. Close out work

When a job is ready to be closed:

- All work orders should be completed or explicitly canceled.
- Checklists and logs should be finished and reviewed.
- Time entries and inventory usage should be reconciled.
- The job status is set to completed.

From there, the job feeds into:

- Invoicing flows (for billing customers).
- Analytics and reporting (for understanding performance and profitability).

## Example: Emergency call from a key customer

Imagine a key customer calls with an emergency:

1. You create a **job** under their existing project and mark it as high priority.
2. You add one or more **work orders** describing what needs to be done.
3. You assign a nearby technician and use the schedule view to slot the visit in as soon as possible.
4. As the technician works, you:
   - See updates to work order statuses.
   - Watch time and notes roll in.
5. Once the job is resolved:
   - You confirm all work orders are completed.
   - You reconcile time and parts.
   - You generate an invoice and, if needed, share a summary of the work done with the customer.

This flow gives you a clear, traceable path from “we have an urgent problem” to “the work is done and billed correctly.”
