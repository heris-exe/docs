## Overview

Jobs and work orders are at the heart of Fieldgrid’s operations model. This guide walks through the **admin job lifecycle**:

- Creating jobs under projects.
- Breaking work into work orders.
- Assigning technicians.
- Tracking progress and closing out work.

## Entry points

Admins usually start from:

- The **Jobs** section in the sidebar.
- Project or customer views that link into filtered lists of jobs.

## Lifecycle steps

### 1. Create or import jobs (planning the work)

From the Jobs list:

1. Click **Create job**.
2. Choose:
   - Which **customer** this job is for.
   - Which **project** it belongs under (or create a new one if needed).
3. Fill out the job details:
   - **Job name** – something technicians and coordinators will recognize quickly.
   - **Description** – enough context so anyone opening the job understands the goal.
   - **Dates** – start date and target completion date.
   - **Status** – for example, planning, active, or on hold.
   - **Priority** – low, medium, high, or urgent.
   - **Lead technician** (if applicable) – the person ultimately responsible for the job.
4. Save the job.

Best practices:

- Use naming patterns that make sense to your team (for example, `[Customer] – [Site] – [Short description]`).
- Capture any special constraints (hours, site rules, safety considerations) in the description so technicians aren’t surprised on arrival.

If you already have a list of jobs in a spreadsheet, you may also be able to **import** multiple jobs at once, mapping columns like customer, project, job name, and dates.

### 2. Define work orders (breaking work into visits/tasks)

Within a job, admins break work into **work orders**:

1. Open the job you just created.
2. Go to the **Work orders** area.
3. For each visit or major task:
   - Give it a **title** (for example, “Site survey – rooftop units”).
   - Add a short **description** of what needs to be done.
   - Choose the **jobsite** (if there are multiple).
   - Set a **due date**, **priority**, and **status** (for example, to-do).
4. Repeat for all work you expect to happen under this job.

For more complex work:

- Add **checklists** so that technicians know exactly what to check, measure, or verify.
- Use **subtasks** if you want to track smaller pieces of work separately while still keeping them under the same work order.

Best practices:

- Treat work orders as **technician-sized chunks of work**—something someone can reasonably complete in a visit.
- Use consistent language for similar types of work so reporting and search stay clean.

### 3. Assign technicians and schedule work (putting the right people in the right place)

Admins can assign technicians either:

1. From the job or work order view:
   - Choose which technician(s) should be responsible.
2. From the **Schedule** view:
   - Drag or drop jobs/work orders into specific dates and times.
   - Adjust assignments as needed when people are off or priorities change.

This ensures each technician knows exactly which work orders they own and when they’re expected to be on site.

Best practices:

- Avoid assigning too many high‑priority work orders to the same technician in a single day.
- Use the schedule to **visualize load** across the team rather than relying on memory.

### 4. Track progress and collaboration (following the work as it happens)

In the job details view, admins can see:

- Which work orders are still to‑do, in progress, or done.
- Which technicians are assigned and where they are working.
- Any time logged against the job.
- Checklists, site logs, comments, and files captured by the team.

This creates a single place to understand how a job is progressing and what, if anything, is blocked.

Tips for staying on top of things:

- Skim recent **logs and comments** to spot risks early.
- Look for work orders that are “stuck” in the same status for too long.
- Use notes and photos as a quick way to confirm that the work matches expectations.

### 5. Close out work (finishing the lifecycle)

When a job is ready to be closed:

- All work orders should be completed or explicitly canceled.
- Checklists and logs should be finished and reviewed.
- Time entries and inventory usage should be reconciled.
- The job status is set to completed.

From there, the job feeds into:

- Invoicing flows (for billing customers).
- Analytics and reporting (for understanding performance and profitability).

## Example: Emergency call from a key customer

Imagine a key customer calls with an emergency:

1. You create a **job** under their existing project and mark it as high priority.
2. You add one or more **work orders** describing what needs to be done.
3. You assign a nearby technician and use the schedule view to slot the visit in as soon as possible.
4. As the technician works, you:
   - See updates to work order statuses.
   - Watch time and notes roll in.
5. Once the job is resolved:
   - You confirm all work orders are completed.
   - You reconcile time and parts.
   - You generate an invoice and, if needed, share a summary of the work done with the customer.

This flow gives you a clear, traceable path from “we have an urgent problem” to “the work is done and billed correctly.”
